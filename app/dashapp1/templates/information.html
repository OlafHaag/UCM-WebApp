The Circle Task

Task Information

Todorov (2004) claimed that a different control law, than one obeying the minimal intervention principle, that acts in all directions could further reduce variance in redundant direction, but only at the expense of increased variance in the task relevant direction. The reason for this would be the increase in control-dependent muscle noise. Large variability in redundant direction would be necessary to achieve optimal performance. If we were to impose an additional task on a degree of freedom that is also part of a redundancy in another task, we should expect an increase in variance in the original task's performance criterion, because of increased variability in the task-relevant direction, if this claim is true.

This analysis uses data acquired with the UCM Research app's Circle Task. It's main purpose is to exercise UCM analysis on a small scale in preparation for more complex analyses. This task mimics the two-finger pressure task (*reference needed). As the circle task is done on a touch device bi-manually, the contributing muscular systems to the task are not directly connected and may receive separate motor commands. Increased control-dependent noise on one side should not affect the other. An increase in the first task criterion in a 2 tasks exercise could be attributed to increased attention/multi-task cost.

When constraint is lifted, redundant degrees of freedom would be uncontrolled again, utilizing more range of motion and therefore, more variance in redundant degrees of freedom again and less variance in performance criterion. No priming effect due to constraints is expected.

Figure 1. An example of a trial with only 1 task. The task is to scale the size of the white disc to match the green ring by using the 2 vertical sliders. The violet outer ring is a visualization of the countdown, which is also displayed in seconds in the middle. If it reaches a full circle, the trial is over. The left slider is degree of freedom 1 (df1) and the right slider is degree of freedom 2 (df2). Both add equally to the size of the white disc. Sliders have a range from 0-100 and start at 10%. To successfully accomplish the task, they need to add up to 125.

Figure 2. An example of a trial with 2 concurrent tasks. In addition to scaling up the white disc, one slider is expanding the blue arc. The extra task goal is to make the blue arc meet the blue dot at the bottom of the green circle. Which slider is controlling the blue arc is once chosen randomly at the beginning of the block. The slider controlling the arc receives a color coding as well. This second task goal imposes a constraint on the chosen degree of freedom to be at 50%.
